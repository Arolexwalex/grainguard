# Aflatoxin risk factors

## What aflatoxins are
Aflatoxins are invisible, tasteless poisons produced by the mould *Aspergillus flavus* and close
relatives. They cannot be detected by sight, smell, or taste — visibly clean grain can still carry
dangerous levels, and only a lab test confirms contamination for certain. Maize and groundnut
are the most vulnerable staple crops; roughly a quarter of the maize and groundnut grown across
Sub-Saharan Africa is estimated to carry some level of contamination.

## Why it matters
Aflatoxin exposure is linked to liver cancer, suppressed immunity, and stunted growth in
children, and can pass from mother to unborn child. Beyond health, contaminated crops lose
commercial value and can be rejected outright by premium buyers and export markets — export
limits across Africa generally sit between 10-20 parts per billion, tighter in the EU.

## Conditions that raise the risk
Contamination can begin in the field and worsens through harvest, drying, and storage.
Risk climbs when several of these are present together:
- Poor soil fertility and crop stress (unfertilised or drought-stressed plants are more
  vulnerable to fungal invasion)
- Insect damage — wounds created by pests like stem borer give the fungus an entry point
- High humidity (above ~85%) combined with high temperatures (above ~25°C) during growth or
  storage
- Delayed harvesting, especially when mature crop is left standing or lying in the field
- Slow or improper drying, or drying directly on bare ground
- Traditional storage structures that don't control humidity or protect against rain

## Reducing the risk — the practical checklist
1. Harvest promptly at maturity — don't let matured crop sit in the field.
2. Remove and discard visibly damaged, insect-eaten, or diseased grain during sorting, both at
   harvest and again during drying.
3. Dry quickly and thoroughly on a clean raised surface to reach safe moisture content
   (see moisture_and_drying.md).
4. Store only fully dried grain, in a clean, ventilated structure, off the bare ground.
5. Never mix newly harvested grain with older stored grain.
6. Where available, biocontrol products such as Aflasafe — which introduce a harmless strain of
   the same fungus to crowd out the toxin-producing strains — can reduce aflatoxin levels by
   80-99% when applied at the correct crop stage (roughly 2-3 weeks before flowering for maize,
   30-45 days after planting for groundnut) and combined with good field and storage practices.

*Sources: IITA Farmers' Guide to Management of Aflatoxins in Maize and Groundnuts in Africa
(2017); Ghana MoFA/CSIR-SARI, Appropriate Postharvest Practices for Improved Grain Storage
(2017).*
