# Cleaning storage containers between harvests

## Safe, accessible practice: sweep and clean before reuse
Before loading a new harvest into any storage container - a PICS bag, a traditional sack, a
crib, or a room used for bulk storage - sweep or vacuum out all old grain debris, insect
remains, and dust first. Leftover material from a previous, possibly infested harvest is one of
the easiest ways a new, clean harvest gets contaminated on day one. This step is simple, safe,
and requires no special equipment or training - it should be treated as a standard step, not an
optional extra.

## Chemical fumigation (phosphine) - important safety note
Phosphine gas fumigation is a real, effective industrial method for eliminating insect
infestations in large-scale grain storage, and has been used since the 1950s. However, it is
**highly toxic and dangerous to handle without proper training and equipment** - it requires
certified applicators, full protective equipment (sealed overalls, gloves, respirators with
gas cartridges), gas-tight structures, and careful monitoring of gas concentration over several
days. It is legally restricted to licensed applicators in most jurisdictions specifically
because of its acute inhalation hazard.

This matters directly for our use case: **most smallholder farmers and even most extension
agents do not have the means, training, or protective equipment to safely self-administer
phosphine fumigation**, and improper use has caused fatalities in documented cases. This is not
a technique to casually suggest as a home remedy - it should only be recommended in the context
of contacting a licensed pest control service or agricultural extension office equipped to
do it safely, never as a do-it-yourself step.

## What this means for advice given to individual farmers
When infestation is severe enough that sweeping, sorting, and hermetic storage aren't enough,
the appropriate recommendation is to **contact a local agricultural extension office or a
licensed fumigation service**, not to describe fumigant handling as if it were a simple home
task. Recommending specific fumigants (phosphine, aluminum/magnesium phosphide) without this
caveat risks real harm, not just inaccuracy.

*Sources: Alabama Cooperative Extension System / Farm Progress reporting on grain bin
fumigation practice; Oklahoma State University Extension safety guidance on phosphine
fumigation hazards and certification requirements; peer-reviewed perspective on phosphine
fumigation access constraints among resource-limited farmers in Ghana (cross-regionally
relevant to Nigeria's smallholder context).*
