# Moisture and drying

## Why moisture matters most
Grain moisture is the single biggest factor in how long stored grain stays safe. Once the air
inside a store reaches above roughly 65-70% relative humidity around the grain, mould and
storage insects begin to thrive. Getting grain below its "safe moisture content" before storage
is the first line of defence against both insect damage and aflatoxin contamination.

## Safe moisture content by crop (approximate, at ~27°C)
| Crop | Safe moisture content (% wet basis) |
|---|---|
| White maize | 13.5% |
| Yellow maize | 13.0% |
| Paddy rice | 14.0% |
| Milled rice | 12.0% |
| Sorghum | 14.0% |
| Millet | 14.5% |
| Cowpea | 14.5% |
| Soybean | 13.5% |
| Bambara groundnut | 14.0% |
| Groundnut (shelled) | 7.0% |

Higher storage temperatures generally call for even lower moisture content. Groundnut needs to
be dried much further than cereals before it is safe to bag.

## How farmers can check moisture without a meter
A digital moisture meter is the most reliable option, but where one isn't available, farmers use
practical field tests:
- **Bite test**: if a grain kernel shatters cleanly between the teeth, it's dry; if it feels sticky
  or squishes, it still holds too much moisture.
- **Shake/rattle test**: dry grain makes a sharp rattling sound when shaken in a closed tin or
  bottle; damp grain sounds dull.
- **Salt-in-bag test**: a small amount of salt sealed in a nylon bag with grain will show visible
  wet patches on the nylon if the grain is not yet dry.

## Drying practice
- Dry grain on a clean, raised surface — a tarpaulin or cemented platform — never directly on
  bare soil, which reintroduces moisture and contamination.
- During drying, sort out and remove any immature, insect-damaged, or visibly diseased grain
  before it mixes with the healthy batch.
- Grain heaped for drying should be shaped so it stays upright rather than spreading or
  collapsing — flat or oversized heaps trap moisture at their centre even when the surface looks
  dry.

*Sources: IITA Farmers' Guide to Management of Aflatoxins in Maize and Groundnuts in Africa
(2017); Ghana MoFA/CSIR-SARI, Appropriate Postharvest Practices for Improved Grain Storage
(2017).*
