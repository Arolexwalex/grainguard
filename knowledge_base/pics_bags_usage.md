# Using PICS bags correctly

PICS (Purdue Improved Crop Storage) bags are a triple-layer system: two inner high-density
polyethylene liners plus one outer woven polypropylene sack. They're one of the most widely
adopted hermetic storage tools across Sub-Saharan Africa, suitable for maize, cowpea, sorghum,
millet, soybean, and groundnut (not paddy rice).

## Before filling
- Only use bags where both inner liners are free of holes or tears — a punctured liner defeats
  the airtight seal and the bag will not work as intended.
- Grain must already be clean (winnowed, debris removed) and dried to safe moisture content
  before it goes in. PICS bags preserve grain condition; they don't correct grain that went in
  too wet.

## Filling and sealing sequence
1. Separate the three bags so they can be filled one at a time.
2. Fill the first (innermost) liner with grain, leaving enough neck at the top to tie it off,
   and gently press to remove trapped air pockets as it fills.
3. Twist, fold, and tie the first liner securely.
4. Pull the second liner up over the first and repeat the same tie-off process.
5. Place both sealed liners inside the outer woven bag and tie that closed too — this outer
   layer provides the physical strength for handling and stacking.

## After sealing
- Store in a cool, dry room, never directly on bare ground — use a raised platform, wooden
  rack, or pallets.
- Avoid opening and reclosing the bag repeatedly, since each opening lets in fresh oxygen and
  moisture.
- Bags can typically be reused for future harvests as long as they remain free of holes.

## Trade-offs to know
- Upfront cost is higher than a basic jute or polypropylene sack, and bags may be hard to find
  in some rural markets.
- Properly used, grain quality holds up for one to three years without any chemical
  treatment, and germination rate for stored seed is largely preserved — a meaningful advantage
  over untreated traditional storage.

*Source: Ghana MoFA/CSIR-SARI, Appropriate Postharvest Practices for Improved Grain Storage
(2017); Purdue University PICS program documentation.*
