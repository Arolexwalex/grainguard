# Storage insect pests

Aflatoxin (covered in aflatoxin_risk_factors.md) is one major storage threat; the other is
direct insect damage, which also indirectly raises aflatoxin risk by creating entry wounds
for mould.

## The main culprit: maize weevil (Sitophilus zeamais)
The maize weevil is consistently identified as one of the most destructive storage pests of
maize worldwide, particularly across tropical and subtropical regions. Reported losses
attributed to this single pest range widely by study — from roughly 15% up to as much as 90%
of a stored harvest in worst-case smallholder conditions, with 40% being a commonly cited
average figure for developing-country contexts.

How it causes damage:
- Adult weevils lay eggs inside individual grain kernels
- Larvae develop and feed from within the kernel, invisible from the outside until damage is
  advanced
- This feeding activity creates small openings in the kernel, which then become entry points
  for fungi and bacteria — directly linking insect infestation to elevated mould and aflatoxin
  risk
- Beyond direct grain loss, infestation reduces germination rate (a problem if the grain is
  meant for next season's seed) and lowers both nutritional and market value

## Control approaches, in order of what a smallholder can realistically access
1. **Prevention through hermetic storage** (PICS bags, sealed containers) — the low-oxygen
   environment suppresses weevil development without any chemical input, and is the most
   consistently recommended first-line defence in the literature reviewed for this project.
2. **Botanical/plant-based control** — various plant extracts have been studied as
   insecticide-free alternatives to synthetic chemicals, motivated partly by growing insect
   resistance to synthetic insecticides and by the health/environmental risks of chemical
   residue on stored food. This is an active research area rather than a single standardised
   practice, so specific botanical recommendations should be flagged as "consult a local
   extension source" rather than given definitively.
3. **Synthetic insecticide dust** — effective but carries resistance-development risk over time
   and health/safety handling concerns; treated as a fallback rather than a first recommendation
   in the source material.

## Practical implication for the advisor
When a user describes visible insect damage or holes in stored grain, the appropriate response
should flag both the direct loss risk and the compounded aflatoxin risk from insect damage —
these two problems reinforce each other and the advice should address both together rather than
treating them as separate issues.

*Sources: peer-reviewed research on Sitophilus zeamais biocontrol and storage damage (2023-2024,
various); cross-referenced against PICS/hermetic storage literature already in this knowledge
