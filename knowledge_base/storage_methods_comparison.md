# Storage methods compared

## The four broad categories
1. **Traditional structures** — mud silos, maize cribs, woven baskets. Cheap and familiar, but
   offer little protection against insects, rodents, or moisture, and are the main reason
   postharvest losses can run as high as a third of a harvest.
2. **Improved traditional methods** — jute or polypropylene sacks, improved mud-silo designs.
   A modest step up, still vulnerable to pests and humidity swings.
3. **Improved small-scale methods** — hermetic (airtight) options like PICS bags, sealed plastic
   drums, metal silos. These are the recommended tier for most smallholders.
4. **Large-scale methods** — metal grain silos, large warehouses — generally out of reach for
   individual smallholders, more relevant to cooperatives or aggregators.

## Why hermetic storage works
Hermetic (airtight) storage doesn't use any chemical — it works by sealing the grain so
completely that insects and microbes inside the container use up the available oxygen and
effectively suffocate themselves, halting further damage without any added pesticide. This also
protects grain that's already dry from picking up new moisture, provided it went in properly
dried.

## General rules that apply across storage types
- Only store grain that has reached safe moisture content — hermetic bags don't fix wet grain,
  they just seal in whatever condition it started in.
- Clean and, where infestation is suspected, fumigate the storage structure before loading a new
  harvest.
- Keep bags or containers off bare floors and away from walls, ideally on a raised
  platform or pallets.
- Never combine a new harvest with old stored grain in the same container.
- Inspect stored grain periodically rather than leaving it completely undisturbed for months.

*Sources: Ghana MoFA/CSIR-SARI, Appropriate Postharvest Practices for Improved Grain Storage
(2017); Purdue University PICS program documentation.*
